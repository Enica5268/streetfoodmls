Char Kway Teow, a popular Malaysian and Singaporean noodle dish, originally developed and catered to overseas-born Chinese labourers in the Southeast Asia region, the dish has achieved widespread popularity within the region from the late 20th century onwards, particularly in Malaysia and Singapore. The dish has also acquired a reputation of being unhealthy due to its high saturated fat content, as it is traditionally stir-fried in pork fat with crisp croutons of pork lard.

In Penang, one of Malaysia‚Äôs famous food capitals, Char Kway Teow is traditionally fried using duck eggs, which gives the dish a richer flavor and silkier texture due to the higher fat content of duck eggs. This is considered a signature touch of Penang-style Char Kuey Teow. In contrast, in most other regions of Malaysia or Singapore, the dish is more commonly prepared using chicken eggs, which are milder in taste and more widely available. While both versions are delicious, Penang‚Äôs use of duck eggs is often praised by food lovers as a premium or more authentic version.

The dish was often sold by fishermen, farmers and cockle-gatherers who doubled as char kway teow hawkers in the evening to supplement their income. The high fat content and low cost of the dish made it attractive to these people as it was a cheap source of energy and nutrients.
